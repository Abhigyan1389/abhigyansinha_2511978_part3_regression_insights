**Model name.** All three share the same dependent variable (monthly_sales) but differ fundamentally in scope. Models 1 and 2 are simple linear regressions with one predictor each. Model 3 is a multiple linear regression with 8 predictors — 4 continuous numerics, 1 binary control, and 3 dummy variables encoding store type.
**Variables used.** Model 1 uses footfall alone. Model 2 uses customer_rating alone (on 312 observations, 8 missing). Model 3 uses footfall, log(marketing_spend), inventory_availability_pct, log(competitor_distance_km), holiday_flag, and three store-type dummy variables with High Street as the baseline.
**R-squared.** The gap between models is stark. Model 1 achieves a strong 73.6% — impressive for a single predictor in cross-sectional retail data. Model 2 achieves 0.08% — essentially zero, with an adjusted R² that goes negative, meaning the model is worse than simply predicting the mean every time. Model 3 reaches 83.5% with adjusted R² of 83.1%, confirming the improvement over Model 1 is genuine and not a result of adding noise predictors.
**Significant variables.** Model 1 has one significant predictor — footfall (p < 0.001, CI entirely above zero). Model 2 has none — the slope for customer rating is statistically indistinguishable from zero (p = 0.622, CI spans −£27,176 to +£16,284). Model 3 has seven of eight predictors significant at α = 0.05: footfall, log(marketing_spend), inventory_availability_pct, and log(competitor_distance_km) at the p < 0.001 level, plus all three store-type dummies. Only holiday_flag falls slightly short at p = 0.064, retained as a control variable given its theoretical importance and the limited 4-month time window.
**Business usefulness**. Model 1 is a useful communication tool — the £35.68 per visitor slope is easy to explain and directionally reliable for footfall planning. Model 2 has no business usefulness whatsoever; any decisions based on it would be no better than guessing the mean. Model 3 is the only model suitable for actual operational and strategic decisions. It quantifies the return on footfall investment, marketing budget, stock availability, and captures the structural sales differences between store types — all simultaneously and while controlling for the others.
**Limitations**. Model 1 suffers from omitted variable bias — because footfall correlates with excluded variables (notably staff count at r = 0.918), its coefficient absorbs some of their effects and is not truly ceteris paribus. Model 2's limitation is the model itself — it has no predictive or explanatory power and should not be used. Model 3's main limitations are the 4-month observation window (which underpowers the holiday coefficient), the 6 rows dropped for missing competitor distance, the inability to capture store-level fixed effects or temporal trends, and the assumption of linearity throughout — all reasonable caveats for a first regression model that can be addressed with more data collection.
