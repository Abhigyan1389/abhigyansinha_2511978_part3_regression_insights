Final Recommendations
**Strongest factors.** Footfall is the dominant driver — an r of 0.858 and a confirmed effect of £33 per additional monthly visitor. Marketing spend and inventory availability are the next two confirmed levers, both at p < 0.001. Store format creates structural differences that persist even after controlling for everything else. Competitor proximity reflects location quality more than competitive dynamics. Holiday periods add roughly £12K in a month, though this estimate is underpowered with four months of data.
**What leadership should focus on.** The three operationally controllable variables are footfall (drive more traffic), inventory availability (eliminate stockouts), and marketing spend (redirect budget to under-invested stores where returns are highest). The structural variables — store type and location — inform benchmarking and site selection rather than day-to-day operations. The residual analysis reveals an underused intelligence layer: the stores outperforming their predictions by £50–£100K are already doing something right that the model cannot see, and those lessons are worth extracting.
**What not to over-interpret.** Customer rating has no relationship with sales and should be dropped from any revenue-related analysis. Average discount percentage is endogenous — struggling stores discount more, which makes it look like discounts cause poor sales when the causality runs the other way. The competitor distance coefficient should not be read as "compete aggressively" — it reflects location quality. Holiday flag should be treated as directional guidance, not a precise forecast.
**Recommended actions**. Six concrete recommendations: a footfall programme at the 20 lowest-traffic stores, a 90% minimum inventory standard, a marketing budget reallocation from high-spending to low-spending stores, site visits to the top 10 positive residual outperformers, performance improvement plans for the persistently underperforming Mall/East stores, and collection of a full 12-month dataset before the next model refresh.
**Risks and limitations.** Four months of data limits seasonality capture and statistical power. The East-West regional bias (−£15K vs +£12K mean residuals) is unexplained by the current model. 16.5% of sales variation remains unexplained — that gap represents real factors (management quality, local reputation, product range) that the model is blind to. The model should be refreshed at minimum annually.
**Association versus causation.** The model identifies what moves together with sales, not what directly causes them. Three reasons matter practically: the data is observational, not experimental — there was no random assignment of marketing budgets; reverse causality is plausible in several directions; and omitted variables inflate the apparent effect of included ones. The practical guidance: treat model coefficients as prioritisation tools and directional expectations, run controlled pilots before committing large budgets to any intervention, and measure actual outcomes against model predictions to test whether the association holds in a causal direction.
